Join the Recycling Movement

Blue bins are a crucial part of modern recycling systems, allowing households and businesses to properly sort and recycle bottles, cans, and other recyclable containers.

By using blue bins correctly, you're helping conserve natural resources, reduce energy consumption, minimize landfill waste, and protect our environment for future generations.

About Blue Bins

Blue bins are designated containers for collecting recyclable materials like bottles and cans. They are typically provided by municipalities as part of their waste diversion and recycling programs.

The recyclables collected in blue bins are sorted, processed, and transformed into new products, reducing the need for virgin materials and lowering the environmental impact of manufacturing.

This sustainable approach helps divert significant amounts of waste from landfills, conserves natural resources, and reduces greenhouse gas emissions associated with producing new materials.

How To Use Your Blue Bin

1

Rinse Containers

Give bottles and cans a quick rinse to remove food residue and liquids. This prevents contamination of other recyclables and reduces odors.

2

Remove Labels and Caps

When required by your local program, remove plastic caps from glass bottles and paper labels. Check your municipal guidelines for specific instructions.

3

Flatten When Possible

Crush aluminum cans and plastic bottles to save space in your bin. This increases collection efficiency and reduces transportation emissions.

4

Sort Properly

Follow your local guidelines for sorting recyclables. Some programs allow commingled recycling while others require separation of different materials.

5

Place for Collection

Put your blue bin out for collection according to your local schedule. Make sure the lid is secure to prevent recyclables from blowing away.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to clean containers before recycling?

Yes, containers should be relatively clean but don't need to be spotless. A quick rinse to remove food residue is sufficient. Excessive food waste can contaminate entire batches of recyclables, potentially causing them to be sent to landfill instead of being recycled.

What happens to the bottles and cans I recycle?

After collection, recyclables are transported to a Material Recovery Facility (MRF) where they're sorted by type. From there, they're processed into raw materials: glass is crushed into cullet, aluminum and steel are melted down, and plastics are shredded and pelletized. These materials are then sold to manufacturers who use them to create new products.

Should I remove labels and caps?

This depends on your local recycling program. Many modern facilities can process bottles with labels and caps attached. However, some programs still request that you remove plastic caps from glass bottles and paper labels when possible. Check your local guidelines for specific instructions.

Can I recycle bottles and cans if I live in an apartment?

Yes! Many multi-unit buildings have blue bin programs. Speak with your property manager or condo board about available recycling options. If your building doesn't offer blue bin service, check if your municipality has community recycling drop-off locations or depots where you can take your recyclables.

What do the numbers on plastic containers mean?

The numbers (1-7) inside the recycling symbol identify the type of plastic resin used in the container. #1 (PET) and #2 (HDPE) are the most widely accepted plastics in recycling programs. Other numbers have varying levels of recyclability depending on local facilities. Always check your local guidelines to confirm which plastic types are accepted in your blue bin.
